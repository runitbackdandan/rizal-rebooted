# Rizal, Rebooted

A short-form video series that annotates a hero the way he once annotated an empire.

## Overview

**Rizal, Rebooted** is a five-episode vertical video series built for social platforms (YouTube Shorts, Reels, TikTok). Each episode takes one real, documented thing José Rizal did or wrote, and stages it in a format his own generation would recognize as an update of what he was already doing.

The series doesn't modernize Rizal by translation. Instead, it argues that Rizal's actual method is already native to short-form video — annotation becomes fact-checking, correspondence becomes a chat thread, a novel's cast becomes a call-out thread.

## Format Specifications

| Spec | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Platforms** | YouTube Shorts, Reels, TikTok |
| **Runtime** | 60–90 seconds per episode |
| **Visual grammar** | Archival scan-texture cut against handheld, phone-shot footage |
| **Target audience** | Filipino students who only meet Rizal as a mandatory subject |

## Series Episodes

### 01 — The Annotator *(Featured this issue)*
Rizal fact-checks a 300-year-old lie about his own country, live, in the margins of Antonio de Morga's colonial account. This episode stages his 1890 annotated edition of *Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas* as a modern fact-check duet.

### 02 — Sa Aking Mga Kabata, Translated
A poem every Filipino child memorizes may not even be his. What that authorship dispute says about how we build heroes.

### 03 — The Group Chat That Never Was
Rizal's real letters to del Pilar and Ponce, restaged as a chaotic, overdue-reply group thread — because that's structurally what they were.

### 04 — Ibarra Deletes His Account
*Noli Me Tangere*'s reformist landlord, recast as an influencer who tried to fix the system from inside it and got quietly cancelled by the same system.

### 05 — Simoun's Burner Account
*El Filibusterismo*'s masked jeweler as a sockpuppet exposé — Rizal's warning about reform curdling into revenge, told as an unmasking video.

## Key Arguments

**Complexity**: Each episode is anchored to a documented act (the annotation project, the correspondence, the novels' real reception) rather than a paraphrase of Rizal's biography — the argument is historiographical, not decorative.

**Craft**: The archive/reboot split mirrors the actual technique the episode is about: keeping the original text visible while a correction runs alongside it.

**Prose**: The premise, quote, and analysis are written to stand on their own as an essay, so the project reads as an argument first and a production plan second.
