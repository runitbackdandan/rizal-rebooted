Editor's Note

Why Rizal doesn't need rewriting

Most school projects "modernize" Rizal by translating him — new slang over old ideas. I wanted to try the opposite bet: that Rizal's actual method is already native to short-form video, and the only thing standing between him and a Gen Z audience is the sixty years of textbook varnish sitting on top of it. He fact-checked a colonial historian in the margins of a 300-year-old book. He ran a correspondence network with del Pilar and Ponce that functioned exactly like a group chat — impatient, funny, occasionally petty, always urgent. He wrote two novels that are, structurally, exposés with a cast of named-and-shamed public figures. None of that needs an update. It needs a platform.

Rizal, Rebooted is a five-episode series built for the feed, not the classroom. Each episode takes one real, documented thing Rizal did or wrote, and stages it in a format his own generation would recognize as an update of what he was already doing: annotation becomes duet-style fact-checking, correspondence becomes a chat thread, a novel's cast becomes a call-out thread. The goal isn't to make Rizal relatable. It's to show that he was already doing the thing we think only we invented.

Deep Dive · Episode 01

The Annotator

In 1888–89, Rizal sat in the British Museum with a copy of Antonio de Morga's Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas (1609) and did something no one had done to a colonial account of the Philippines before: he footnoted it, page by page, correcting its Spanish framing with Filipino sources and his own historical reasoning, and republished the whole annotated edition in 1890. He wasn't writing a new history. He was correcting someone else's, in public, with receipts. That is, functionally, a fact-check duet. This episode stages it as one.

Why this one carries the weight of the series

The Morga annotation is the least "novelistic" thing Rizal made, and the most radical. Annotating a primary source to contest its framing — rather than simply writing a rebuttal essay — is a historiographical move: it keeps the original claim visible while surrounding it with contradiction, so the reader watches the correction happen instead of just being told the conclusion. That's precisely the mechanic of a modern fact-check duet, which succeeds or fails on whether it keeps the original clip on screen. Rizal understood, a century before the format existed, that correcting a narrative works better in the presence of the narrative than in its absence. Episode 01 isn't a costume drama about a historical figure doing something admirable. It's a claim that a specific rhetorical technique — annotate, don't just argue — has a Filipino origin point most students are never shown.

Reading This as a Project

What the format is doing

Complexity

Each episode is anchored to a documented act (the annotation project, the correspondence, the novels' real reception) rather than a paraphrase of Rizal's biography — the argument is historiographical, not decorative.

Craft

The archive/reboot split isn't a visual gimmick — it mirrors the actual technique the episode is about: keeping the original text visible while a correction runs alongside it.

Prose

The premise, quote, and analysis are written to stand on their own as an essay, so the project reads as an argument first and a production plan second.
